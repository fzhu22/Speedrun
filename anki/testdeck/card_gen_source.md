## Enzymes as catalysts

Enzymes are biological catalysts, and almost all enzymes are proteins (a few are RNA
molecules called ribozymes). A catalyst speeds up a reaction by lowering its activation
energy without being consumed and without changing the equilibrium position of the
reaction. Because the catalyst is not consumed, a single enzyme molecule can turn over
many substrate molecules. Enzymes do not change the free-energy difference (delta G)
between reactants and products; they change only the rate at which equilibrium is reached.

The reactant an enzyme acts on is called the substrate. The substrate binds at the active
site. The "induced fit" model states that the active site changes shape slightly to fit
the substrate once it binds, which is a refinement of the older "lock and key" model.

## Factors that affect enzyme activity

Each enzyme has an optimal temperature and an optimal pH at which its rate is highest. In
humans most enzymes work best near 37 degrees Celsius and near pH 7.4, but pepsin in the
stomach works best around pH 2. Raising the temperature above the optimum denatures the
enzyme: it loses its three-dimensional shape and therefore its activity. Denaturation
changes shape but does not break the peptide bonds of the primary sequence.

A cofactor is a non-protein helper an enzyme needs to work. Inorganic cofactors are often
metal ions such as zinc or magnesium. An organic cofactor is called a coenzyme, and many
coenzymes are derived from vitamins; for example NAD+ is derived from niacin. An enzyme
without its cofactor is an apoenzyme, and the complete, active enzyme with its cofactor is
a holoenzyme.

## Inhibition

A competitive inhibitor resembles the substrate and binds the active site, so it can be
overcome by adding more substrate; it raises the apparent Km but leaves Vmax unchanged. A
noncompetitive inhibitor binds a separate allosteric site, cannot be overcome by more
substrate, and lowers Vmax while leaving Km unchanged. Feedback inhibition is when the end
product of a pathway inhibits an earlier enzyme in that same pathway.

## Michaelis-Menten kinetics

Km is the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax. A low Km
means the enzyme reaches half-maximal speed at low substrate concentration, so a low Km
indicates high affinity for the substrate. Vmax is the maximum rate, reached when the
enzyme is saturated with substrate.

## Bioenergetics and ATP

The cell's main short-term energy currency is ATP (adenosine triphosphate). Energy is
released when the bond to the terminal phosphate is hydrolysed, converting ATP to ADP plus
inorganic phosphate. A reaction that releases free energy is exergonic and has a negative
delta G; a reaction that absorbs free energy is endergonic and has a positive delta G.
Cells drive endergonic reactions by coupling them to the exergonic hydrolysis of ATP.

Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm and converts one glucose into two pyruvate
molecules, with a net yield of two ATP and two NADH. Glycolysis does not require oxygen.
In aerobic conditions pyruvate enters the mitochondrion; the citric acid cycle runs in the
mitochondrial matrix, and the electron transport chain is embedded in the inner
mitochondrial membrane. In the electron transport chain the final electron acceptor is
oxygen, which is reduced to water. Most ATP in aerobic respiration is produced by
oxidative phosphorylation, driven by a proton gradient across the inner membrane in a
process called chemiosmosis.

Under anaerobic conditions in human muscle, pyruvate is reduced to lactate, which
regenerates the NAD+ needed to keep glycolysis running.
