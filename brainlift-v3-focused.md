# Learning-Science Features for an MCAT Anki Fork — Focused Edition: The 3 Strongest, Deeply Evidenced

## Owners

Franklin Zhu

This focused edition keeps only the three strongest of the seventeen Spiky Points of View (SPOVs) developed in the full second version of this BrainLift; the other fourteen are deliberately pruned for focus, not because they failed any gate, and each is logged in `v3-focused/rejected.md`. A "Spiky Point of View" is a prescriptive, defensible, non-obvious stance that tells a builder what to do and carries the experiment that would settle it. To earn a place here, each of the three surviving positions was put through a dedicated deepening research wave — an advocate (evidence for), an adversary (counter-evidence and boundaries), and a frontier scout (current-year sources plus a re-verification of Anki's engineering reality), run across different model families so their blind spots do not correlate — that backs every individual component of the claim. The new facts then passed a citation gate (zero facts dropped; three wording fixes applied), and the three positions were re-tested through the full three-test protocol: Test 1 (cold spikiness, judged by fresh Claude and GPT models that see only the claim), Test 2 (a GPT red-teamer that extracts the single load-bearing claim and probes its depth), and Test 3 (external verification of that claim against primary sources). One honest reframing fell out of this work and is carried openly below: SPOV 10 and SPOV 13 are so thoroughly established in the learning-science literature that they are **not** spiky against expert consensus at all — the deep evidence base is precisely *why* they are not contrarian among scientists. Their spike is against **current market practice**: the evidence supports them, but the MCAT-prep and Anki ecosystem does not actually ship them. SPOV 4 is different — it is spiky against both expert framing and the market, and it is the one **Validated** position because its load-bearing claim is already settled against primary evidence (Anki's own source code).

## Headline

Build three: the two-tier architecture (settled), plus per-family support-fading and pretest-first cards — proven learning science the MCAT market does not yet ship.

## Purpose

**Purpose (North Star).** Of the many learning-science features a team could build into a fork of Anki (the open-source spaced-repetition flashcard application) aimed at the Medical College Admission Test (MCAT), this edition answers a narrower and harder question than the full menu does: *which very few features are the most worth building, and how certain can we actually be about each one?* It trades the breadth of the seventeen-position menu for depth on three, defending each one component by component against primary sources, naming the boundary where each stops holding, and stating the single concrete check or experiment that would resolve whatever uncertainty remains. The deliverable is reusable build context — not an implementation — so a colleague can read it, see exactly what is settled versus pending, and decide what to build first.

### In Scope
- **SPOV 4 — the two-tier architecture** (a desktop "prepare" pass that emits small syncable artifacts, then a cross-platform "review" pass), and its buildability against Anki's real substrate (the Rust `rslib` engine, the global custom-scheduling JavaScript surface, the `customData` store, and the AnkiMobile/AnkiDroid client constraints).
- **SPOV 10 — fading scaffolding within a per-concept-family mastery estimate**, its mechanism (cognitive load theory and the expertise-reversal effect), and its buildability as desktop-prepared state surfaced through the review tier.
- **SPOV 13 — a forced "guess → reveal → in-session feedback" first-exposure card mode** (pretesting / errorful generation), its mechanism, and its buildability on the same two-tier substrate.

### Out of Scope
- **The fixed project givens** (fork Anki; keep a shared Rust engine; remain MCAT-specific) — these are constraints, not debated here.
- **The fourteen pruned SPOVs** from the full version (student-authored card scaffolds, deep-structure sort gates, curated discrimination drills, the metrics layer, multiple-choice feedback engines, confidence routing, non-distorting gamification, the AI lane, exam-date desired-retention ramps, the CARS module decision, the test-anxiety non-feature, the figure/data reasoning module, spaced-generative refutation, and guided drawing). They remain candidate-valid; they are simply not re-defended here and are recorded in `v3-focused/rejected.md`.
- **Non-MCAT exams** (USMLE, the bar exam, NCLEX, the LSAT/GRE) — they appear only as out-of-distribution "reach" checks that test whether a claim generalizes, never as targets.

**Tier legend.** Two tiers appear in this edition. **Validated** means the position's single load-bearing claim (its "crux") is already confirmed against primary evidence while the claim is still off-frame — a true-but-not-yet-acted-on fact, the strongest a position can be. **Strong** means all three tests pass and the mechanism is established across primary sources, but the decisive, MCAT-specific magnitude still pends one named experiment.

**Spikiness frames.** Spikiness is always relative to an audience. SPOV 4 is spiky against **both expert framing and current market practice**. SPOV 10 and SPOV 13 are spiky against **current market practice only** — the science is mainstream, but MCAT-prep and Anki tools do not ship it.

### SPOV 4 — The two-tier architecture (Validated)

**Spiky POV 4 (Validated):** Every concept-aware feature must be built in two tiers — a desktop "prepare" pass that sees the whole collection and emits a small, syncable *semantic* artifact, followed by a cross-platform "review" pass — because stock Anki, AnkiMobile, and AnkiDroid cannot perform live, on-device **semantic** re-ranking of the due queue by concept **without a pre-prepared concept artifact**; any promise that "your phone reorders your study queue by concept in real time" is, on the stock engine, vaporware.

Spiky relative to: **expert framing and current market practice.** There is no academic consensus mandating a two-tier build, so the architectural prescription is off-consensus for experts; and study-app marketing routinely implies live, on-device, concept-smart sequencing, which the engine cannot actually deliver without desktop preparation.

The case, component by component:
- **Stock custom scheduling cannot reorder the due queue (verified on current `main`, 2026).** Anki's scheduler protocol defines `SchedulingContext { string deck_name = 1; uint64 seed = 2; }` — exactly two fields, a deck name and a random seed (ankitects/anki, `proto/anki/scheduler.proto`, 2026 — https://github.com/ankitects/anki/blob/main/proto/anki/scheduler.proto). The function that builds this context populates only the deck name and the card's review seed; the queue itself (new / learning / review) is assembled on the Rust backend and delivered as a pre-built `QueuedCards` set, and the custom JavaScript runs per-card *at answer time* over the already-computed `states`, never over queue assembly (ankitects/anki, `rslib/src/scheduler/queue/mod.rs`, 2026). The official v3-scheduler FAQ confirms the custom JavaScript "applies to the entire collection," is calculated in advance per card, and runs on AnkiMobile and AnkiDroid (AnkiWeb FAQ — https://faqs.ankiweb.net/the-2021-scheduler.html). The load-bearing consequence: the JavaScript can re-weight a single card's next interval, but it cannot reorder which cards the backend already chose to show.
- **Persisted scheduler state is tiny.** The validator `validate_custom_data` requires keys of at most 8 bytes ("custom data keys must be <= 8 bytes") and a serialized JSON payload of at most 100 bytes; the limit is measured in *bytes*, not characters, so `{"日":5}` passes while `{"日本語":5}` fails, and it exists because larger values slow scans of the cards table (ankitects/anki, `rslib/src/storage/card/data.rs` — path corrected from the second version's `rslib/data.rs`). A fork therefore cannot stash a rich per-card concept model in `customData`; the semantic work has to live in a prepared artifact.
- **Mobile clients expose JavaScript only — no Python add-ons.** AnkiMobile's sole scheduling-customization surface is the custom-scheduling JavaScript, and iOS exposes no Python add-on system at all (AnkiMobile docs — https://docs.ankimobile.net/study-tools.html). AnkiDroid's custom JavaScript scheduling runs as Kotlin/Java inside a webview (AnkiDroid, commit edbddfe, 2023-08-08). The desktop-only "prepare" tier is the only place rich logic can run.
- **The v3 scheduler cannot be monkey-patched.** The FAQ is explicit: "It is no longer possible to selectively replace parts of the scheduler's code ('monkey patching')," and v3 has been the default and only scheduler since Anki and AnkiMobile 23.10 and AnkiDroid 2.17; the development forum echoes that since v3 most scheduling logic lives in Rust and can no longer be modified by add-ons (AnkiWeb FAQ — https://faqs.ankiweb.net/the-2021-scheduler.html). A fork cannot quietly graft semantic re-ranking into the existing scheduler.
- **The exposed surface in 2026 — with a READ-versus-WRITE nuance.** Pull request #4880, "Expose card's decay and DR to custom scheduler," merged on 2026-06-08 and shipped in release 26.05, now exposes a card's decay and desired retention (the FSRS target probability of recall) for **reading** inside custom-scheduling JavaScript (ankitects/anki, PR #4880 — https://github.com/ankitects/anki/pull/4880; release 26.05 notes). Per-deck desired retention shipped earlier, in release 25.09 (PR #4194, 2025-09-06 — https://github.com/ankitects/anki/releases/tag/25.09). However, a native per-card desired-retention **write** from JavaScript is still unmerged (a community fork pull request, last pushed 2025-12-05), so per-card desired retention remains settable only per deck today (Anki forums — https://forums.ankiweb.net/t/allow-custom-scheduling-js-to-modify-card-desired-retention/67701). This corrects a wording slip in the prior version, which said per-card desired retention is "unaccessible to custom scheduling": after #4880 that is outdated for *reading* (writing is still desktop/add-on only). Crucially, this nuance does not touch the load-bearing claim — being able to read a card's decay does not let the JavaScript reorder the backend-built queue.
- **No falsifier exists.** The adversary searched for a counterexample and found none: stock Anki, AnkiMobile, and AnkiDroid cannot perform live, on-device, semantic queue re-ranking by concept without a desktop-prepared artifact. The only genuine nuance is that the *reorder action itself* — rebuilding a filtered deck, running custom study, or repositioning cards — can execute on-device, but only over tags and fields that already exist plus a built-in sort order; the semantic clustering that decides *what to group* must still be prepared on the desktop. A 2026 forum feature request confirms users still find no way to reorder the queue within a session.
- **Why smart sequencing needs whole-collection context (the learning primitive underneath).** The reason this matters for learning, not just engineering, is that adaptive interleaving — the sequencing that helps most — requires identifying transitions among similar categories across the *entire* category set, which is exactly the whole-collection view only the desktop tier has; both interleaving conditions beat blocked practice immediately and after a delay (Abel, de Bruin, Onan & Roelle, 2025, n=259 — https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lindif.2025.102804). Boundary, kept beside the support: in that same study adaptive interleaving did **not** beat *random* interleaving and did not reduce confusion errors, so the lesson is "sequencing needs the global view," not "any clever reordering wins."

Prediction (or Disconfirmer): **A buildability claim, checkable now and to be re-verified by 2026-12-31.** No working build exists in which live AnkiMobile or AnkiDroid custom-scheduling JavaScript **reorders** the due queue by **semantic concept cluster** — not merely re-weights one card's next interval — using only `{deck_name, seed}` plus `customData` of at most 100 bytes and **with no desktop-prepared concept artifact**. Re-weighting a single interval, rebuilding a filtered deck over pre-existing tags, or producing a front-side template effect does **not** count as a semantic queue re-rank. If such a build is demonstrated, "two-tier is mandatory" is false.

How to resolve it: **Already resolved by primary source — re-runnable today, no experiment required.** Read Anki's `proto/anki/scheduler.proto` (the context exposes only `{deck_name, seed}`), `rslib/src/storage/card/data.rs` (the `customData` 100-byte / 8-byte-key limit), and the v3-scheduler FAQ (the due queue is built on the backend; custom JavaScript runs only at answer time on the current card), and confirm that a native per-card desired-retention *write* from JavaScript is still unmerged on the forums. Because this is a buildability claim rather than an efficacy claim, there are no experimental comparators to assemble; the only ongoing task is to re-verify against `main` as the engine evolves.

Validation: **Test 1 (frame: expert + market) — split pass.** The GPT tester scored it 6/10 "split" (the two-tier *mandate* is a debatable architectural prescription that is off academic consensus), and the Claude tester rated it WEAK (it reads as an absolute engineering claim); net pass, because it is off-consensus enough and clearly spiky against a market that implies live on-device re-ranking. **Test 2 — pass with hardening.** The extracted crux is, "Stock AnkiMobile/AnkiDroid cannot perform live semantic due-queue re-ranking by concept using only on-device surfaces without a desktop-prepared semantic artifact." Retreat-resistance passes: the escape routes are blocked because "re-ranking" explicitly excludes interval re-weighting, filtered-deck rebuilds over pre-existing tags, and template-only effects. Reach passes (any cross-platform spaced-repetition fork that wants on-device semantic re-ranking inherits the same limit). The adopted hardening is to always say "semantic" and "without a pre-existing concept artifact," and to concede that on-device actions over already-prepared tags and fields are allowed. **Test 3 — HOLDS-ESTABLISHED** against Anki's `main` source tree plus the releases (citation gate verified), with the PR #4880 read-access update folded in. This is the jackpot case: a true-but-not-widely-known buildability fact, settled by primary source, on which the other two positions physically depend.

### SPOV 10 — Fade support per concept-family (Strong)

**Spiky POV 10 (Strong):** Difficulty should fade *scaffolding* within a per-concept-family **demonstrated-mastery estimate** — never via a global "hard mode," and never applied to declarative recall — and the per-family mastery estimator itself must be treated as the *tested* component, using conservative thresholds, reinstating support the moment the learner misses, and excluding verbal and rote material.

Spiky relative to: **current market practice.** This is the honest reframing: among learning scientists, mastery-gated fading of support and the expertise-reversal effect are mainstream, so the position is *not* expert-spiky. What no shipping MCAT tool does is the actual feature: AnKing decks, UWorld, and Jack Westin offer fixed cards or, at most, a single global difficulty setting — none fade scaffolding per concept-family on a demonstrated-mastery estimate.

The case, component by component:
- **The expertise-reversal effect is real, large, and freshly meta-analyzed.** The newest meta-analysis (Tetzlaff, Simonsmeier, Peters & Brod, 2025, *Learning and Instruction* v98: 176 effect sizes across 60 studies, N=5,924) finds that low-prior-knowledge learners learn better with *high* instructional assistance (d=0.505) while high-prior-knowledge learners learn better with *low* assistance (d=−0.428), an overall reversal contrast of d=0.971, 95% CI [0.631, 1.312] (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2025.102142). Boundary, kept beside the support: the effect is **asymmetric** — helping novices (d=0.505) is a stronger and more reliable move than withholding help from experts (d=−0.428) — and it is "less clear" for younger students and for humanities and language content. This is the newest such meta as of mid-2026.
- **The mechanism is cognitive load, specifically element interactivity.** Expertise reversal is a variant of the element-interactivity effect (Chen, Kalyuga & Sweller, 2017 — https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-016-9359-1); "element interactivity" refers to how many pieces of information a learner must hold in mind and relate at once, and it is the defining mechanism of intrinsic cognitive load (Sweller, 2010). The guidance-by-element-interactivity interaction is statistically significant, F(1,45)=5.16, p=.028, partial η²=.103: higher guidance helps high-element-interactivity material, lower guidance helps low-element material (Chen, Kalyuga & Sweller, 2016 — https://doi.org/10.1017/edp.2016.16). Many cognitive-load effects disappear or reverse on low-element-interactivity material such as memorizing isolated formulae (Sweller et al., 2023), which is exactly why this position scopes itself away from rote and declarative recall.
- **Fading plus principle prompts buys transfer at no extra study time.** Backward fading of worked examples — progressively removing solution steps from the last step back — improved both near transfer (f=.23) and far transfer (f=.27), and self-explanation prompts improved near (f=.25) and far (f=.23) transfer, while total study time did not differ between conditions (p=.95 and .42) (Atkinson, Renkl & Merrill, 2003 — https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.95.4.774). Fading fosters near transfer, and backward fading beats forward fading (Renkl et al., 2002 — https://doi.org/10.1080/00220970209599510). Boundary: far transfer was not reliably produced, so the realistic promise is durable near transfer, not a far-transfer miracle.
- **Mastery-gated, per-skill (adaptive) fading beats both fixed fading and a global setting.** When fading was gated on per-theorem mastery — faded once a Bayesian Knowledge Tracing estimate of mastery reached ≥.7 and reinstated below .5 — it beat both fixed fading and plain problem-solving: lab effects of d=.49 immediate and d=.59 delayed, and a classroom delayed effect of d=.74 (and d=.91 against problem-solving), while using fewer worked steps (d=1.25) and no extra time, with mastery estimated **separately per theorem/skill**, not as a single global level (Salden, Aleven, Schwonke & Renkl, 2010 — https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-009-9107-8). Adaptive task selection that chooses the next item from prior-problem assistance beat a fixed sequence (Najar, Mitrovic & McLaren, 2016), and adaptive fading that advances only after a successful attempt beat static fading at no extra time, with the effect large for lower-ability learners (Cohen's f≈0.68) but non-significant for higher-ability learners (JEE, 2006). At the meta-analytic level, AI-driven adaptive personalization shows g=0.70 across 45 studies (Wu et al., 2024 — https://doi.org/10.1177/07356331241240459) and an overall 0.490 across 31 studies (knowledge outcomes 0.557; Wang et al., 2024). The pattern ships today in tools that gate on mastery estimates: Carnegie Learning's MATHia (Bayesian Knowledge Tracing mastery; a RAND randomized trial found roughly 0.20 standard deviations), ALEKS (Knowledge Space Theory, mastery-based, re-testing for retention), and Khan Academy mastery levels [practitioner / measured-secondary].
- **Boundaries and counter-evidence — the estimator is the soft point.** The single most contestable component is the *dynamic per-family expertise estimate* itself. A field randomized trial found adaptive practice did **not** beat static practice, and static practice actually won for high-ability students by about +0.08 standard deviations (van Klaveren et al., 2017 — https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econedurev.2017.04.003). Bayesian and deep knowledge-tracing mastery estimates are data-hungry, can degenerate, are unstable and order-sensitive, and tend to over-predict mastery — so a fork that fades on a noisy per-family estimate could fade too early. Separately, AI-tutoring gains concentrate in procedural and hierarchical skills (roughly 0.2–0.3 standard deviations) and narrow or disappear for open-ended reasoning, which is a real boundary for MCAT reasoning and for the Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS) section (2024–2026 practitioner synthesis). These are reasons to *test* the estimator, not to assume it.

Prediction (or Disconfirmer): In a volume-matched and time-matched randomized trial reporting by **2027-09-01**, per-family support-fading will beat **both** a global "hard mode" arm and a no-fading arm on unseen, high-element-interactivity MCAT science items by at least 0.3 standard deviations, **and** will show approximately zero or negative effect on declarative-recall families. The claim is non-retreatable: if a global hard mode matches per-family fading on unseen high-element items at equal time, "fade per family, don't escalate globally" fails; if fading also helps declarative recall, the high-element scoping was wrong.

How to resolve it: Run that volume-matched randomized trial, and — per the Test 2 hardening — it must include the full comparator set, or the result does not bind. Required arms and controls: a **static-fading** arm (not just no-fading), a **global-hard-mode** arm, a **high-ability subgroup** (because static practice can win there), and **declarative-recall and humanities/language/CARS control families** (where the effect should vanish). Use conservative mastery thresholds and reinstate support on misses, and report the *incremental* lift of the per-family estimator over static fading specifically — that delta, on MCAT science, is the one quantity still unproven.

Validation: **Test 1 — frame-dependent.** Against the **expert** frame it fails / is weak: the GPT tester nodded along at 8/10 (mastery-gating, fading-with-competence, and desirable difficulty are mainstream, and "never" is overstrong), and the Claude tester rated it WEAK. Against the **market** frame it passes cleanly: there is a clear gap, since AnKing, UWorld, and Jack Westin ship fixed cards or a global difficulty setting, not per-family mastery-gated fading. The adopted frame is therefore **market**. **Test 2 — pass with hardening.** The crux is, "A per-concept-family demonstrated-mastery estimator can identify readiness to fade support better than a static/fixed sequence or a global difficulty setting on high-element MCAT science families." Retreat-resistance passes *only if* the resolving trial carries the static-fading, global-hard-mode, high-ability, verbal/CARS, and declarative-recall controls listed above. Reach passes (the same logic governs math and procedural tutoring; it does not extend to vocabulary). The hardening makes the per-family estimator the explicitly tested component — conservative thresholds, reinstatement on misses, exclusion of declarative and verbal material, and a static-fading comparator — because van Klaveren (2017) shows adaptive can fail to beat static and knowledge-tracing estimates can be unstable. **Test 3 — HOLDS-PENDING.** Established against primary sources: the expertise-reversal effect (Tetzlaff 2025, d=0.505 / −0.428), element interactivity, fading-plus-prompts at no extra time (Atkinson & Renkl, 2003), and adaptive-beats-fixed fading (Salden 2010, d=.49/.59/.74). Pending, and named as the contested point: the per-family-estimator-beats-static delta on MCAT science, where estimator reliability is the soft component.

### SPOV 13 — Pretest-first card mode (Strong)

**Spiky POV 13 (Strong):** Introduce brand-new material through a forced "commit a guess → reveal the answer → mandatory in-session corrective feedback" mode (pretesting, also called errorful generation), inverting the spaced-repetition default of "only review what you already know" — and bank the gain to the **specific pretested item** by default, relaxing that rule only when the exact card format is shown to spill over to the surrounding topic.

Spiky relative to: **current market practice.** Again the honest reframing: pretesting with immediate feedback and an item-specific benefit is standard learning science, so this is **not** expert-spiky. The gap is in the market: spaced-repetition tools default to deferring or suspending what the learner does not yet know and to a study-then-test order, and they do not force a guess-then-feedback first exposure.

The case, component by component:
- **The pretesting (prequestion) effect is well-established, and it is item-specific.** A meta-analysis found a specific benefit of g=0.54 (k=97) for prequestioned content versus only g=0.04 (k=91) for the surrounding, non-prequestioned content, consistent with an attention-based account (St. Hilaire et al., 2023/2024 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-023-02353-8; PubMed 37640836). A 2025 meta-analysis replicated the pattern: prequestions help specific content (g=.66) but not general content (g=.01), and feedback-plus-prequestions beats prequestions alone (King-Shepard et al., 2025, *Educational Psychology Review* 37:115 — https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-025-10075-7). A field study found that after seven days, pre-testing helped the *trained* questions (d=0.35) but not untrained ones (d=−0.01), while post-testing helped both (trained, and untrained d=0.36) (Latimier et al., 2019, *npj Science of Learning* — https://doi.org/10.1038/s41539-019-0053-1). Boundary, kept beside the support: some prequestion formats *do* produce topic-wide (general) benefits — multiple-choice prequestions (Little & Bjork, 2016 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-016-0621-z) and a placement/order effect that widens the attentional window (Sana & Carpenter, 2023 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-023-02274-6) — so the strict "item-specific only" banking rule is format-dependent, which is exactly why this position banks to the item *by default* rather than as a universal law.
- **Feedback is necessary, not optional.** Without corrective feedback, the testing effect is essentially zero at low initial accuracy: g=0.03 at ≤50% initial accuracy without feedback, versus g=0.73 with feedback (Rowland, 2014 — https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037559). Prequestioning *with* feedback improved learning, while *without* feedback it produced no significant gain (Yu, Heng, Arden-Gardner, Ding & Pan, 2025, n=87 — https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2025.102187). Because a first-exposure guess is wrong much of the time, mandatory in-session feedback is the load-bearing requirement, not a nicety.
- **Errors must be semantically proximate to the target.** An unsuccessful retrieval attempt followed by feedback enhanced learning for *related* materials (Kornell, Hays & Bjork, 2009, Experiment 4, d=0.38 at equal time — https://doi.org/10.1037/a0015729). Error generation helped related word pairs but not unrelated ones (Grimaldi & Karpicke, reported via Mera et al., 2021 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-021-02022-8), and a close guess beats a distant guess, with non-proximate guessing sometimes worse than plain study (Seabrooke et al., 2021 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-021-01218-6). The design implication is concrete: the lures and the guess space must be near the target, or the mode backfires.
- **Learners systematically undervalue it (a metacognitive illusion).** Errorful generation plus feedback beat reading, yet learners judged it *less* effective — their judgments of learning differed from the actual outcome, F=7.78, p=.002 (Potts & Shanks, 2014 — https://doi.org/10.1037/a0033194), a misalignment replicated by Yang, Potts & Shanks (2017 — https://doi.org/10.1037/xlm0000364), and participants consistently underestimated testing efficacy again in a 2025 study (*Memory & Cognition*). This matters for product design: because users will not *choose* a mode they wrongly believe is worse, the guess-then-feedback structure should be the default first-exposure flow rather than an opt-in.
- **It holds in authentic classrooms — and the head-to-head against post-testing is genuinely material-dependent.** In a large authentic-classroom study (30 classes, 1,571 students), delayed performance was higher for prequestioned items (73.7% versus 68.8%; odds ratio 1.28), with 83% of classes directionally better, though prequestions slightly reduced video viewing (Motz et al., 2025, "ManyClasses 2" — https://doi.org/10.1037/edu0000978). A review concludes that pre-testing enhances memory and transfer *when learners study the correct answers afterward* (Pan & Carpenter, 2023 — https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-023-09814-5). The contested part — whether pretesting *beats* test-after-study — is honestly mixed and material-dependent: pretesting beat post-testing for expository prose (Pan & Sana, 2021, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied* 27(2):237–257, n=1,573 — https://doi.org/10.1037/xap0000345), but post-testing beat pre-testing for trained questions (Latimier, 2019, d=0.74 versus 0.35), and for word pairs pretest, posttest, and copy-test-copy were statistically indistinguishable (*Memory & Cognition*, 2025 — https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-025-01813-x). The citation gate explicitly bars claiming "pretest beats post-testing for prose" as settled; the honest claim is that pretest is at least *competitive* with post-testing and adds an item-specific encoding benefit. A 2026 preprint adds that pretesting alone is insufficient for long-term retention and is best preserved when followed by structured, spaced retrieval [preprint-unreviewed], which fits treating the pretest as a first-exposure catalyst that the FSRS scheduler then consolidates. Two further cautions from the adversary: pretesting raised dropout and disengagement in some massive-open-online-course settings (Janelli & Lipnevich, 2020 — https://doi.org/10.1016/j.compedu.2020.104076; their 2024 N≈6,100 replication found no persistence effect, so the hedge "in some settings" is correct), and multiple-choice lures can create persistent false knowledge. The feature ships in adjacent products today: guess-with-feedback matching in language apps (a 2026 *Cognitive Research* replication, d=0.25–0.67 — https://doi.org/10.1186/s41235-026-00708-y) and Unisium's "try before you know" pretest-mode spaced repetition [practitioner].

Prediction (or Disconfirmer): In a volume-matched and time-matched randomized trial reporting by **2027-09-01**, introducing new cards via forced-guess-then-feedback will beat study-first on delayed, unseen-style tests **of those specific items** by g≥0.3, **and** will show approximately zero advantage on non-pretested items in the same topic. The claim cannot retreat to "testing beats restudy": a **test-after-study arm is mandatory**, and if post-testing matches the pretest mode at equal time, the claim collapses to "test, don't restudy." If the benefit spreads to untested items, the default item-specific banking rule was wrong for that format.

How to resolve it: Run a three-arm, equal-time randomized trial — study-first versus pretest-then-feedback versus test-after-study — with feedback held constant across arms, and, per the Test 2 hardening, with two non-negotiable design constraints: the **test-after-study arm** is a required comparator (because the pretest-versus-posttest head-to-head is contested and material-dependent), and **only semantically proximate lures** may be used (because errorful generation backfires for unrelated pairs). Measure delayed, unseen-style accuracy on the pretested items plus a same-topic, non-pretested control set, and track dropout and friction (the massive-open-online-course disengagement signal).

Validation: **Test 1 — frame-dependent.** Against the **expert** frame it fails / is weak (the GPT tester nodded along at 8/10 — pretesting with immediate feedback and item-specific credit is standard learning science — and Claude rated it WEAK). Against the **market** frame it passes (a clear gap: spaced-repetition tools default to reviewing what the learner already knows and do not force a guess-then-feedback first exposure). The adopted frame is **market**. **Test 2 — pass with hardening.** The crux is, "Forced guess → reveal → immediate feedback improves delayed performance on the exact pretested item over study-first AND stays competitive with test-after-study, when errors and lures are semantically proximate." Retreat-resistance passes *only* once "credit the gain only on the specific item" is softened to "default-bank the item unless the exact format proves spillover." Reach passes (the same structure helps compliance and rule onboarding). The hardening adds the mandatory test-after-study comparator, bans non-proximate lures, tracks dropout and friction, and treats item-specificity as the default accounting rule rather than a universal law (it is format-dependent: Little & Bjork, 2016; Sana & Carpenter, 2023). **Test 3 — HOLDS-PENDING.** Established against primary sources: the item-specific gain (King-Shepard 2025, g=.66/.01; St. Hilaire 2023, g=.54/.04) and feedback-dependence (Rowland 2014; Yu 2025). Pending and contested: whether the pretest mode beats, or merely matches, test-after-study (material-dependent), and the format-dependence of item-specificity.

## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree

This is a focused knowledge tree covering only the evidence for the three positions above. It is organized by the engineering substrate for SPOV 4, the difficulty-and-scaffolding evidence for SPOV 10, and the first-exposure pretesting evidence for SPOV 13. ### A. Engineering substrate (SPOV 4)

#### A1. Anki scheduler, primitives, and cross-platform limits
- **Source — Anki source tree and v3-scheduler FAQ.** *DOK 1 facts:* the scheduling context exposes only `SchedulingContext{deck_name, seed}`; the due queue (new / learning / review) is built on the Rust backend and delivered as `QueuedCards`, while the custom JavaScript runs at answer time over the current card's pre-built `states`; the custom scheduling is global JavaScript applied to the entire collection and runs on AnkiMobile and AnkiDroid; the v3 scheduler cannot be monkey-patched and has been default-and-only since 23.10 (AnkiDroid 2.17); `customData` is capped at 100 bytes with 8-byte keys (bytes, not characters), because larger values slow cards-table scans; AnkiMobile allows no add-ons and AnkiDroid allows no Python (JavaScript only). *DOK 2 summary:* the "obvious" sequencing features are built in, but live on-device *semantic* re-ranking of the queue is not possible on the stock engine — concept-aware sequencing must be desktop-prepared and surfaced through the review tier. *Link to source:* https://github.com/ankitects/anki/blob/main/proto/anki/scheduler.proto ; https://faqs.ankiweb.net/the-2021-scheduler.html ; https://docs.ankimobile.net/study-tools.html
- **Source — FSRS exposed-surface releases (PR #4880; release 25.09).** *DOK 1 facts:* PR #4880, "Expose card's decay and DR to custom scheduler," merged 2026-06-08 and shipped in release 26.05, exposing per-card decay and desired retention for **reading** in custom-scheduling JavaScript; per-deck desired retention shipped in release 25.09 (PR #4194, 2025-09-06); a native per-card desired-retention **write** from JavaScript is still unmerged (community fork pull request, last pushed 2025-12-05), so per-card desired retention is settable per deck only. *DOK 2 summary:* the read surface widened in 2026, but writing per-card targets and reordering the queue still require desktop preparation — confirming, not weakening, the two-tier mandate. *Link to source:* https://github.com/ankitects/anki/pull/4880 ; https://github.com/ankitects/anki/releases/tag/25.09 ; https://forums.ankiweb.net/t/allow-custom-scheduling-js-to-modify-card-desired-retention/67701

#### A2. Why smart sequencing needs whole-collection context
- **Source — Adaptive interleaving (Abel, de Bruin, Onan & Roelle, 2025).** *DOK 1 facts:* adaptive interleaving required identifying transitions among similar categories across the whole category set; both interleaving conditions beat blocked practice immediately and after a delay (n=259). *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* adaptive interleaving did **not** beat random interleaving and did not reduce confusion errors. *DOK 2 summary:* effective sequencing needs the global, whole-collection view that only the desktop "prepare" tier has, which is the learning reason behind the engineering mandate; the boundary is that "global view" matters more than any specific clever reorder. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lindif.2025.102804

### B. Difficulty, scaffolding, and expertise (SPOV 10)

#### B1. Expertise reversal and element interactivity
- **Source — Expertise-reversal meta-analysis (Tetzlaff, Simonsmeier, Peters & Brod, 2025).** *DOK 1 facts:* 176 effect sizes, 60 studies, N=5,924; low-prior learners do better with high assistance (d=0.505), high-prior learners better with low assistance (d=−0.428); reversal contrast d=0.971, 95% CI [0.631, 1.312]. *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* the effect is asymmetric (helping novices is the stronger, more reliable direction) and "less clear" for younger students and for humanities/language. *DOK 2 summary:* scaffolding should be high for novices and faded as competence grows; a global "hard mode" actively hurts the already-competent. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2025.102142
- **Source — Element interactivity / cognitive load (Chen, Kalyuga & Sweller, 2016/2017; Sweller, 2010/2023).** *DOK 1 facts:* expertise reversal is a variant of the element-interactivity effect; the guidance-by-element-interactivity interaction is significant, F(1,45)=5.16, p=.028, partial η²=.103 (higher guidance helps high-element material, lower guidance helps low-element material); element interactivity is the defining mechanism of intrinsic load. *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* many cognitive-load effects disappear or reverse on low-element-interactivity material such as memorizing isolated formulae. *DOK 2 summary:* the fade-support logic applies to high-element-interactivity, problem-solving content and should be scoped *away* from declarative and rote recall. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-016-9359-1 ; https://doi.org/10.1017/edp.2016.16

#### B2. Worked-example fading plus principle prompts
- **Source — Fading and self-explanation (Atkinson, Renkl & Merrill, 2003; Renkl et al., 2002).** *DOK 1 facts:* backward fading improved near (f=.23) and far (f=.27) transfer; self-explanation prompts improved near (f=.25) and far (f=.23) transfer; study time did not differ (p=.95/.42); fading fosters near transfer and backward fading beats forward fading. *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* far transfer was not reliably produced (Renkl et al., 2002). *DOK 2 summary:* fading scaffolds while prompting the underlying principle yields durable near transfer at no extra time — the practical shape of the fade ladder. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.95.4.774 ; https://doi.org/10.1080/00220970209599510

#### B3. Adaptive (mastery-gated) versus fixed fading
- **Source — Adaptive worked-example fading (Salden, Aleven, Schwonke & Renkl, 2010; Najar et al., 2016; JEE 2006; adaptive-personalization metas; shipping tutors).** *DOK 1 facts:* mastery-gated fading (faded at per-theorem mastery ≥.7, reinstated <.5) beat fixed fading and problem-solving — lab d=.49 immediate / .59 delayed; classroom delayed d=.74 (d=.91 versus problem-solving); fewer worked steps (d=1.25); no extra time; mastery estimated separately per skill; adaptive task selection beat a fixed sequence; adaptive fading beat static fading, large for lower-ability (f≈0.68) but non-significant for higher-ability; AI-adaptive personalization g=0.70 across 45 studies (Wu et al., 2024) and 0.490 across 31 studies (Wang et al., 2024); MATHia (BKT mastery; RAND ~0.20 SD), ALEKS, and Khan mastery ship the pattern [practitioner / measured-secondary]. *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* a field trial found adaptive practice did not beat static, with static winning for high-ability students by ~+0.08σ (van Klaveren et al., 2017); BKT/DKT mastery estimates are data-hungry, unstable, order-sensitive, and over-predict mastery; AI-tutoring gains narrow or disappear for open-ended reasoning. *DOK 2 summary:* mastery-gated, per-skill fading beats fixed fading and a global setting where the estimator is sound — but the per-family estimator is the soft component and must be tested against a static-fading comparator. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-009-9107-8 ; https://doi.org/10.1177/07356331241240459 ; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econedurev.2017.04.003

### C. First-exposure pretesting and errorful generation (SPOV 13)

#### C1. Pretesting effect and item-specificity
- **Source — Pretesting meta-analyses and field study (St. Hilaire et al., 2023; King-Shepard et al., 2025; Latimier et al., 2019).** *DOK 1 facts:* prequestioned content g=0.54 (k=97) versus general g=0.04 (k=91); 2025 meta specific g=.66 / general g=.01, with feedback-plus-prequestions > prequestions alone; field study after 7 days trained d=0.35 / untrained d=−0.01 (pre-testing), trained-and-untrained positive for post-testing. *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* some formats yield topic-wide benefits — multiple-choice prequestions (Little & Bjork, 2016) and a placement/order attentional-window effect (Sana & Carpenter, 2023) — so "item-specific only" is format-dependent. *DOK 2 summary:* the pretesting benefit is real and concentrates on the pretested item, so banking to the item is the right default, relaxed only where a format proves spillover. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-023-02353-8 ; https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-025-10075-7 ; https://doi.org/10.1038/s41539-019-0053-1 ; https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-016-0621-z ; https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-023-02274-6
- **Source — Feedback-dependence (Rowland, 2014; Yu et al., 2025).** *DOK 1 facts:* no-feedback testing effect g=0.03 at ≤50% initial accuracy versus g=0.73 with feedback; prequestioning with feedback improved learning while without feedback there was no significant gain (n=87). *DOK 2 summary:* because first-exposure guesses are frequently wrong, mandatory in-session corrective feedback is load-bearing, not optional. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037559 ; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2025.102187

#### C2. Error proximity and the metacognitive illusion
- **Source — Error proximity (Kornell, Hays & Bjork, 2009; Grimaldi & Karpicke via Mera et al., 2021; Seabrooke et al., 2021).** *DOK 1 facts:* unsuccessful retrieval plus feedback enhanced learning for related materials (d=0.38 at equal time); error generation helped related but not unrelated pairs; a close guess beats a distant guess, and non-proximate guessing can be worse than study. *DOK 2 summary:* the guess space and lures must be semantically near the target, or errorful generation backfires — a hard design constraint. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1037/a0015729 ; https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-021-02022-8 ; https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-021-01218-6
- **Source — Metacognitive illusion (Potts & Shanks, 2014; Yang, Potts & Shanks, 2017).** *DOK 1 facts:* errorful generation plus feedback beat reading, yet learners judged it less effective (judgments of learning differed, F=7.78, p=.002); the misalignment replicates, and a 2025 study again found learners underestimate testing efficacy. *DOK 2 summary:* learners will not voluntarily choose a mode they wrongly believe is worse, so the guess-then-feedback flow should be the default first exposure. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1037/a0033194 ; https://doi.org/10.1037/xlm0000364

#### C3. Authentic-classroom evidence and the contested head-to-head
- **Source — Classroom and head-to-head (Motz et al., 2025; Pan & Carpenter, 2023; Pan & Sana, 2021; Memory & Cognition, 2025; massive-open-online-course studies; shipping products).** *DOK 1 facts:* delayed performance higher for prequestioned items (73.7% vs 68.8%; odds ratio 1.28), 83% of classes directionally better, with a small reduction in video viewing (ManyClasses 2; 30 classes, 1,571 students); pretesting enhances memory and transfer when learners study the correct answers afterward; pretest beat posttest for expository prose (n=1,573). *Counter-evidence (kept beside):* post-testing beat pre-testing for trained questions (Latimier, 2019, d=0.74 vs 0.35), and pretest ≈ posttest ≈ copy-test-copy for word pairs (2025) — so the pretest-versus-posttest head-to-head is material-dependent (the citation gate bars claiming pretest beats post-testing for prose as settled); pretesting raised dropout/disengagement in some massive-open-online-course settings (Janelli & Lipnevich, 2020; 2024 replication found no persistence effect); multiple-choice lures can create persistent false knowledge; a 2026 preprint notes pretesting alone is insufficient without structured spaced retrieval afterward [preprint-unreviewed]; guess-with-feedback ships in language apps (d=0.25–0.67) and Unisium [practitioner]. *DOK 2 summary:* the pretest mode is a strong first-exposure catalyst that spaced repetition then consolidates, but the test-after-study arm is essential — the honest claim is "at least competitive with post-testing, plus an item-specific encoding benefit," not a clean win. *Link to source:* https://doi.org/10.1037/edu0000978 ; https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-023-09814-5 ; https://doi.org/10.1037/xap0000345 ; https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-025-01813-x ; https://doi.org/10.1016/j.compedu.2020.104076 ; https://doi.org/10.1186/s41235-026-00708-y
